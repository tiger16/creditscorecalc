What builds your score

FICO weighs five factors — two of them dominate.

35%

Payment History

A single 30-day late payment can drop your score by 60–110 points. On-time payments are the single biggest lever you control.

30%

Credit Utilization

The share of your credit limit you're using. Keep it under 30% — ideally under 10% — for the best scores.

15%

Length of History

Older accounts help. Avoid closing your oldest cards even if you rarely use them.

10%

Credit Mix

Having both revolving (cards) and installment (loans) accounts shows lenders you can manage different debt types.

10%

New Credit Inquiries

Each hard inquiry (a lender checking your credit for an application) can trim 5–10 points. They fade after 12 months.

How to raise it — fast

Ranked by how quickly you'll see results.

TIP 01

Pay down card balances

Reducing utilization below 10% can add 20–50 points within one billing cycle. It's the fastest move available.

Impact: High · Speed: 30 days
TIP 02

Dispute errors on your report

One in five reports has an error. Dispute via AnnualCreditReport.com — bureaus must respond within 30 days.

Impact: High · Speed: 30–60 days
TIP 03

Become an authorized user

Ask a family member with an old, low-utilization card to add you. Their history can appear on your report immediately.

Impact: Medium · Speed: 30 days
TIP 04

Request a credit limit increase

Same balance, higher limit = lower utilization percentage. Call your card issuer — often approved without a hard pull.

Impact: Medium · Speed: Immediate
TIP 05

Set up autopay for minimums

Missed payments are the #1 score killer. Autopay for at least the minimum on every account eliminates the risk entirely.

Impact: High · Speed: Ongoing
TIP 06

Don't close old accounts

Closing a card reduces your available credit and can shorten your average account age — both lower your score.

Impact: Medium · Speed: Preventive

Common questions

How often does my credit score update? ▼
Lenders report to bureaus roughly once a month — usually on your statement closing date. So your score typically refreshes monthly, though the exact timing varies by creditor.
Does checking my own score hurt it? ▼
No. Checking your own score is a "soft inquiry" and has zero effect. Only hard inquiries — when a lender checks your credit for an application — can affect your score.
How long do negative items stay on my report? ▼
Most negative items (late payments, collections, charge-offs) stay for 7 years from the date of first delinquency. Bankruptcies can remain for 7–10 years depending on the type.
What's the difference between FICO and VantageScore? ▼
Both use a 300–850 scale and similar factors, but they weigh them differently. FICO is used in roughly 90% of lending decisions. VantageScore is more commonly used in free monitoring tools. A good score on one is generally a good score on the other.
Can I build credit with no credit history? ▼
Yes — a secured credit card (backed by a cash deposit) is the most reliable starting point. Use it for small purchases and pay in full each month. You can typically graduate to an unsecured card within 12–18 months.
